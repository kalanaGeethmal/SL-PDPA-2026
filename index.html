Scope — Who does this apply to?

The Act covers any processing of personal data where: (a) processing takes place wholly or partly within Sri Lanka; or (b) the controller/processor is domiciled, incorporated, or established in Sri Lanka; offers goods or services to people in Sri Lanka; or specifically monitors behaviour of people in Sri Lanka.

It does not apply to purely personal or household use of data, or to data that is not personal data. The 2025 amendment narrowed the definition of "public authority" — public corporations and Companies Act companies are no longer classified as public authorities under PDPA.

Key definitions
Personal Data
Any information that identifies a data subject directly or indirectly — by name, ID number, financial data, location, or factors specific to physical, physiological, genetic, psychological, economic, cultural or social identity.
Controller
Any natural/legal person or entity that determines the purposes and means of processing. Sole or joint. Responsible for all obligations under Part I. Not to be confused with a processor.
Processor
A separate entity that processes data on behalf of a controller, under the controller's written instructions. Example: Hospital A stores patient records with Cloud Provider B — B is the processor. Employees of the controller are not processors.
Special Categories
Racial/ethnic origin, political opinions, religious/philosophical beliefs, genetic data, biometric data (for unique identification), health data, sex life/orientation, criminal data, and data relating to children. Requires stricter lawful basis.
Profiling
Processing personal data to evaluate, analyse or predict aspects concerning a data subject's performance, economic situation, health, preferences, interests, credibility, behaviour, habits, location or movements.
Consent
Freely given, specific, informed and unambiguous indication — by written declaration or affirmative action — that the data subject agrees to processing. Cannot be bundled with unrelated contract terms. Always revocable.
Third Country (2025)
Any territory or country other than Sri Lanka. Simplified by 2025 amendment — the old adequacy decision framework was replaced by a compliance-and-safeguards model.

Seven controller obligations (Sections 5–11)
S.5
Process lawfully
Must satisfy a condition in Schedule I, II, III, or IV
There are six lawful bases in Schedule I: (a) consent, (b) contract performance, (c) legal obligation, (d) vital interests/emergency, (e) public task, and (f) legitimate interests. For special categories, Schedule II applies — stricter. Consent under Schedule III must meet four conditions: demonstrable, clearly distinguishable, free of charge to withdraw, and subject must be informed of the right to withdraw before consenting.
AI practitioner note
Using training data scraped from public sources may only be lawful under "legitimate interests" — but this is overridden if the data subject's interests take precedence. Do not assume public data is freely usable. Biometric data for model training requires Schedule II basis.
S.6
Define and stick to purpose
Purpose must be specified, explicit, and legitimate
Collected data cannot be reused for incompatible purposes. Exceptions exist for archiving, scientific research, historical research, and statistics — but these require appropriate safeguards under Section 10.
AI practitioner note
Training an AI model on customer data collected for "service delivery" may breach this if the model serves a different purpose. Always document the ML use case as a specific purpose at collection time, or obtain fresh consent before repurposing data.
S.7
Data minimisation
Adequate, relevant, and proportionate only
Collect and process only what is necessary for the defined purpose. There is no obligation to collect more data simply because it might improve processing outcomes.
AI practitioner note
Model performance is not a justification for collecting excess data. If you can achieve your task with anonymised or aggregated data, you may be required to do so. Document your minimum viable data set.
S.8
Ensure accuracy
Keep up to date; erase or rectify without undue delay
Every reasonable step must be taken to erase or rectify inaccurate or outdated data. "Undue delay" is not defined in the Act — the Authority will define standards via guidelines.
AI practitioner note
ML models trained on stale data may produce decisions based on outdated personal information. If your model uses personal data as features, you must keep a mechanism to update or remove stale records from training pipelines.
S.9
Limit retention
No longer than necessary for the defined purpose
Data may not be retained in identifiable form beyond what is needed. Research and archiving exceptions exist but require appropriate safeguards (Section 10).
AI practitioner note
Retaining a labelled training dataset indefinitely is a compliance risk. Define and document a retention schedule. Consider whether the trained model itself constitutes "stored personal data" — this is a live legal question across jurisdictions.
S.10
Integrity and confidentiality
Encryption, pseudonymisation, anonymisation, access controls
Technical and organisational measures must prevent unauthorised or unlawful processing, and prevent loss, destruction, or damage. The Act explicitly names encryption, pseudonymisation, anonymisation, and access controls as valid measures.
AI practitioner note
Model outputs that reveal personal data (e.g. memorisation in LLMs) may breach this. Anonymisation of training data must be genuine — re-identifiable pseudonymised data is still personal data. Federated learning and differential privacy are relevant technical controls.
S.11
Transparency
Provide Schedule V information in clear, accessible form
Data subjects must receive: controller identity, DPO contact, purposes and legal basis, special categories being collected, consent withdrawal procedure, recipients and third parties, cross-border transfer info, retention period, rights and appeal information, whether provision is mandatory or optional, and details of any automated decision-making including profiling logic.
AI practitioner note
If your system makes automated decisions, you must disclose "reasonably meaningful information about the logic involved." This is a direct obligation to provide AI explainability. Black-box models deployed against Sri Lankan data subjects may breach this provision.
Data Protection Management Programme (Section 12)

Every controller must implement a DPMP — an internal programme that maintains catalogued records of how obligations are met, runs internal oversight, handles complaints and breach identification, conducts DPIAs, and facilitates data subject rights. The programme must be updated periodically. The 2025 amendment removed the requirement for the Authority to issue guidelines on DPMP directly — this is now handled under new Section 51A's general guidelines power.

Data Protection Officer (Section 20)

A DPO is mandatory for: (a) any Ministry or government department; or (b) controllers/processors whose core activities involve systematic monitoring at scale, special categories at scale, or categories of processing that carry risk of harm as determined by the Authority. The 2025 amendment expanded the DPO definition to include third-party DPOs not directly employed by the controller or processor.

Data Protection Impact Assessment (Section 24)

A DPIA is required before processing that involves: (a) systematic and extensive evaluation of personal data or special categories including profiling; (b) systematic monitoring of publicly accessible areas or telecom networks; or (c) activities prescribed by rules based on scope and risk. The DPIA must contain the proposed safeguards. The 2025 amendment changed submission: the controller now only submits the DPIA to the Authority on written request — not mandatorily in advance.

Data Breach Notification (Section 23)

Controllers must notify the Authority of personal data breaches. The form, timing, and circumstances — including when data subjects themselves must be notified — will be defined by rules made by the Authority. No specific mandatory timeline is stated in the Act itself; watch for rules from the DPA.

Five core rights for data subjects
👁
Right of access
Section 13 · Schedule V
The data subject may request confirmation of whether their data is processed, and obtain the full Schedule V information set. The controller must respond in writing within one month. The request is now free of charge under the 2025 amendment. The 2025 amendment also explicitly added Section 18 (automated decision review) to the list of requests covered by Section 17's response procedure.
Response: 1 month · Extendable to 3 months · Free of charge (2025)
↩
Right to withdraw consent & object to processing
Section 14
Where processing is based on consent (Schedule I(a) or Schedule II(a)), the data subject may withdraw at any time — withdrawal does not affect lawfulness of prior processing. Where processing is based on public task or legitimate interests (Schedule I(e) or (f)), the data subject may request the controller to refrain from further processing. The 2025 amendment clarified the appeal grounds here.
Response: 1 month · Free of charge (2025)
✏️
Right to rectification
Section 15
Inaccurate or incomplete data must be corrected without undue delay on written request. The controller is not required to collect new data it would not otherwise process. If the data is held for evidentiary purposes under law, the controller must refrain from further processing it without first rectifying.
Response: 1 month · Free of charge (2025)
🗑
Right to erasure
Section 16
Data subjects may request erasure where: processing was carried out in contravention of obligations (Sections 5–11), the data subject withdraws consent, or a court or written law requires erasure. The controller may refuse on grounds including national security, legal proceedings, or technical feasibility — but must document reasons and inform the DPA on request.
Response: 1 month · Free of charge (2025)
🤖
Right to review automated decisions
Section 18 · Amended 2025
Where a controller makes a decision based solely on automated processing that creates or is likely to create an irreversible and continuous impact on the data subject's rights under the Constitution or any written law, the data subject may request a review. The 2025 amendment added "Constitution" to the scope — previously it only referenced "written law." Exceptions: where the automated decision is authorised by written law, authorised by the Authority, based on the data subject's own consent, or necessary for a contract — subject to Authority safeguards. This exception does not apply to special categories of personal data.
Response: 1 month · Free of charge (2025) · Now includes Constitutional rights
Who can exercise rights? (Section 17(5))

Rights may be exercised by: the data subject themselves; parents or legal guardians where the data subject is a minor; a court-appointed guardian where the data subject is physically or mentally unfit; a person duly authorised in writing; or an heir of a deceased data subject within 10 years of the date of death.

When can a controller refuse? (Section 17(2))

Grounds for refusal: national security, public order, an ongoing investigation or criminal proceedings, prevention/detection/prosecution of offences, rights of other persons, technical or operational infeasibility, inability to establish the data subject's identity, or a legal requirement to continue processing. All refusals must be recorded. The DPA may request these records.

Appeals (Section 19 · Rewritten 2025)

Where a controller refuses any request under Sections 13–18, the data subject may appeal to the Data Protection Authority. The Authority will investigate and determine whether the refusal was lawful — and inform both parties with reasons. Any party aggrieved by the DPA's decision may further appeal to the Court of Appeal within 30 days.

Data collection & preparation

S.5 + Sch.IEvery dataset used must have a valid lawful basis. Consent (Sch.I(a)) requires freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous agreement. Public data does not automatically satisfy this. Scraping Sri Lankan data from the web may only be lawful under legitimate interests — and only if data subject interests do not override.
S.6The purpose of processing must be defined before collection. Collecting data "for future AI use" is not a specific or explicit purpose. Document the intended ML task with precision.
S.5 + Sch.IIBiometric data (facial images, iris data, fingerprints), genetic data, health data, racial/ethnic data — all are special categories. Training a facial recognition or health prediction model requires a Sch.II lawful basis, not just Sch.I.
Sch.IIIWhere consent is the lawful basis, you must demonstrate consent was given, cannot bundle it with contract acceptance, must allow withdrawal at any time, and must inform subjects of their withdrawal right before collecting.
S.11 + Sch.VAt collection time, data subjects must be told: the purpose, the legal basis, the categories of data, any profiling or automated decision-making, the retention period, and the right to withdraw consent. If collecting indirectly (web scrape, data broker), you must notify within one month.

Model training & evaluation

S.7Only the data necessary for the specific ML task may be used. Adding additional features "because they might improve accuracy" without specific justification may breach the data minimisation obligation. Feature selection must be documented and defensible.
S.9Retention of labelled training datasets must have a defined end point. Indefinitely holding personal data in training corpora is non-compliant. Build a data retention and deletion schedule into your ML pipeline documentation.
S.10Training data must be secured — encryption at rest, access controls, pseudonymisation where feasible. A model that memorises and can reproduce personal data (e.g. a generative model) may constitute a breach of integrity and confidentiality obligations. Test for memorisation.
S.8If a data subject exercises the right to rectification, your training data pipeline may need to update or remove that record. Consider whether re-training is required after corrections. Document how accuracy obligations are satisfied across training data updates.
S.12Your DPMP must include records of all processing activities — including ML training runs that use personal data. This is an audit trail requirement. Log what data was used, for what purpose, under which lawful basis, and for how long it was retained.

Deployment & inference

S.24A DPIA is mandatory before deploying a system that involves systematic and extensive evaluation or profiling of personal data. This includes most production ML models that score, rank, or classify individuals. Run the DPIA before deployment, not after.
S.20If your system's core activity involves systematic monitoring at scale or special categories at scale, a Data Protection Officer must be designated. The DPO reviews DPIAs and advises on compliance. They can now be a third-party contractor under the 2025 amendment.
S.11 + Sch.V(m)At deployment, if the system makes automated decisions affecting users, you must proactively disclose the existence of automated decision-making, the logic involved, and the significance and consequences of such processing. This is a direct explainability obligation. Explainability is not optional.
S.23If a model is breached or produces outputs that constitute unauthorised disclosure of personal data, this is a data breach and must be reported to the DPA. Build breach detection into your inference pipeline monitoring.

Operation & automated decisions

S.18 (2025)Where a system makes decisions based solely on automated processing that create an irreversible and continuous impact on data subject rights (under the Constitution or written law), data subjects have the right to request a human review. Credit decisions, hiring filters, insurance pricing, and public service eligibility are clear examples.
S.18(2)The automated decision review right does not apply where: the decision is authorised by law, authorised by the Authority, based on data subject consent, or necessary for contract performance — but safeguards set by the Authority still apply in all cases. Note: this exception is excluded for special categories of personal data.
Sch.I(f) def.Profiling — using ML to evaluate performance, economic situation, health, preferences, credibility, behaviour, habits, or location — must have a lawful basis, respect data minimisation, and satisfy transparency requirements. Document every feature used in a profiling model against these obligations.
S.15, S.16When a data subject exercises rights to rectification or erasure, your system must be capable of acting on these requests without undue delay. For production ML systems, define what "rectification" and "erasure" mean — including in any downstream model predictions based on that data.
S.17(1) 2025Responses to data subject requests must be provided within one month. Extensions are possible (to a maximum of three months total) where reasons are given. All responses and extensions must be free of charge. Build a request management workflow into operations.

Cross-border & cloud processing

S.26 (2025)Cross-border data flows are now governed by a compliance-and-safeguards model. The controller or processor must ensure compliance with all obligations under the Act, and adopt instruments specified by the DPA to ensure binding commitments from the overseas recipient. The old adequacy decision framework has been removed.
S.26(3)Even without full safeguard instruments, cross-border transfer is permitted where: the data subject explicitly consents after being informed of the risks; it is necessary for a contract; it is for legal claims; it is in the public interest; it is an emergency; or it is a pure transit of data through Sri Lanka.
S.26(4)+(5)Public authorities face additional restriction: the Minister may prescribe categories of personal data that public authorities cannot transfer cross-border. This directly affects government digital transformation projects using foreign cloud ML platforms.
S.21+S.22Where a cloud provider processes Sri Lankan personal data on behalf of a Sri Lankan controller, a written contract covering purpose, duration, nature, data type, and obligations is mandatory. The cloud provider must process only on written instructions. If the cloud provider acts outside those instructions, it becomes a controller itself.
S.22(3)+(4)If a cloud provider subcontracts processing (e.g. to an inference API provider), the same obligations apply to the sub-processor. The primary processor remains liable for the sub-processor's compliance failures. Review your cloud vendor chains carefully.

The Personal Data Protection (Amendment) Act No. 22 of 2025 was certified on 30 October 2025. Below are the ten most significant changes and their practical impact.

Changed
Commencement — flexible Ministerial power replaces fixed windows
Section 1(3) was rewritten. The original Act required commencement no earlier than 18 months and no later than 36 months from Speaker certification. The amendment removes these windows and gives the Minister full discretion to appoint commencement dates by Gazette Order. Subsections (4) and (5) were repealed entirely.
Impact · S.1 · Affects all stakeholders — removes certainty on implementation timelines
Changed
Response to data subject requests — 1 month, extendable, free
Section 17(1) was rewritten. The original 21 working-day window is replaced by one calendar month (extendable by two more months with notice). Subsection (6) now makes all requests free of charge by default — the original fee-charging provision was removed. The Authority may later define exceptions by rules.
Impact · S.17 · Systems and operational teams must build request workflows to meet 1-month deadline
Changed
Automated decisions now cover Constitutional rights
Section 18(1) previously covered decisions with impact on rights under "any written law." The 2025 amendment adds "the Constitution." This expands the right to human review to cover fundamental rights decisions (Article 12 equality, Article 14 freedoms) made by automated systems.
Impact · S.18 · Critical for government and public sector AI systems
Changed
DPO scope — third-party DPOs now permitted; public corporations excluded
Section 20 was amended on two dimensions. First, DPOs can now be third parties not directly employed by the controller or processor. Second, the mandatory DPO requirement for "public corporations" (subsection 1(a)) was narrowed to Ministries and government departments only — public corporations are no longer automatically required to appoint a DPO under this provision.
Impact · S.20 · Opens market for DPO-as-a-service; reduces burden on public corporations
Changed
DPIA submission — now on-request only
Section 24(5) previously required controllers to submit DPIAs proactively to the DPA. The amendment changes this to submission only on written request from the Authority. This reduces administrative burden while preserving the DPA's oversight power.
Impact · S.24 · Lowers compliance cost; DPA retains review rights
Removed
Prior consultation — provisions substantially removed
Section 25 originally had seven subsections requiring controllers to consult the Authority where DPIA risks could not be mitigated. The amendment repeals subsections (2) through (6) — removing the formal prior consultation mechanism. The national security processing provision in subsection (7) is retained but simplified.
Impact · S.25 · Reduces regulatory bottleneck before high-risk processing
Changed
Cross-border data flows — adequacy decision framework replaced
Section 26 was completely rewritten. The original framework distinguished between "adequacy decision" countries (Minister-prescribed) and others. The 2025 version removes this distinction entirely. All cross-border flows must comply with the Act's obligations plus DPA-specified instruments ensuring binding commitments from foreign recipients. Public authorities face additional restrictions via Ministerial prescription.
Impact · S.26 · Major change — simplifies framework but requires due diligence on all foreign data recipients
New
New Section 51A — Authority's guidelines power formalised
A new Section 51A was inserted giving the Authority an express power to issue guidelines on any matter specified in the Act, including sectoral guidelines formulated by advisory committees and DPMP matters. This consolidates the guidelines function that was previously scattered across individual sections.
Impact · S.51A · Watch for sectoral guidelines — these will shape compliance in banking, health, telecom
Changed
Definition of "public authority" narrowed
The 2025 amendment redefines "public authority" to mean only Ministries, Departments, Provincial Councils, local authorities, and Ministry/Department branches of Provincial Councils. It explicitly excludes: (a) public corporations; and (b) companies incorporated under the Companies Act No. 7 of 2007. This affects which entities face stricter cross-border restrictions and mandatory DPO requirements.
Impact · S.56 · State-owned enterprises and government companies now have less restrictive obligations
Changed
First rules deadline extended — 24 months → 36 months
Section 52(2) was amended to extend the deadline for the Authority to make its first rules from 24 months to 36 months from the date of coming into operation of Part V. This gives the DPA more time to consult and develop procedural rules for data subject rights and refusal criteria.
Impact · S.52 · Watch the Gazette — operational rules on rights procedures will define practical compliance
